CSS '98

Panel: When Ontogeny Inverts Phylogeny:
            Regulation in a Fully-Connected World?

Panelist/Moderator:

Michael M. Krieger, Bronson, Bronson & McKinnon LLP

Michael M. Krieger is a Los Angeles attorney whose practice is devoted to computer, communication and high technology business transactions and strategic intellectual property protection, especially for software and Internet-related development, acquisition and licensing. He is a frequent speaker and author on computer law/business topics, including domain name/trademark tensions, cryptographic policy, and software contracting. Mr. Krieger chairs the Computer Law Committee of the California Bar's Intellectual Property Section and is an editor of the Section's journal, New Matter.

With degrees from Caltech (B.S.) and UCLA (Ph.D.), Mr. Krieger spent 15 years on the computer science and mathematics faculties of UCLA, MIT, and, as a Fulbright Scholar, UFMG, Brazil, prior to receiving a J.D. from UCLA and joining the California Bar. Mr. Krieger is Of Counsel to Bronson, Bronson & McKinnon, LLP.

Panelists:

Michael D. Scott, Perkins Coie LLP (Los Angeles and Menlo Park, California)

Michael D. Scott received his B.S. in mathematics from the Massachusetts Institute of Technology in 1967, and spent six years doing software system design at the MIT Computation Center, NASA-Electronics Research Lab, Computer Sciences Corporation and Univac before entering law school. He graduated with a J.D. from UCLA in 1974.

Mr. Scott has spent the last 24 years practicing computer law. He has extensive experience in intellectual property matters, particularly as they apply to computer software, licensing, international transactions, multimedia and Internet law. He is author of "Scott on Computer Law" (2d edition), "Scott on Multimedia Law" (2d ed.), and co-author of "The Year 2000 Computer Crisis: Law, Business, Technology (1998). He is also Editor-in-chief of the "Cyberspace Lawyer" and "E-Commerce Law Report" newsletters.

Mr. Scott is a member of the Board of Directors of the Computer Law Association and Chairman of the annual Cyberspace Law School conference and the Year 2000 Legal Issues conference.

Clark Savage Turner, UC Irvine

Clark Savage Turner holds a B.S. (math, 1979) and an M.A. (math, 1981). While teaching mathematics and programming, he studied law, receiving his J.D. (1986). While practicing law in 1988 and 1989, he worked with Nancy Leveson investigating the Therac-25 accidents and went on to receive an M.S. (Information and Computer Science, 1992). He now studies with Professor Debra Richardson in the area of Software Engineering at the University of California, Irvine where he expects to receive his Ph.D. during the coming year.
