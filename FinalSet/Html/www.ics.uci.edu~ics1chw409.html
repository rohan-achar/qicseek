Introduction:

I will be conducting research on the Jewish Holocaust. As an Armenian, I am well aware of the Armenian Genocide of 1915, during whcih 1.5 million Armenians were slaughtered in Turkey. In the beginnings of World War II, Hitler gave a speech to a group of military men describing his plan for the "Final Solution" for the Jewish Question. In that speech he said that the Nazi plan for the Jews would go relatively unnoticed by the international community: Hitler asked rhetorically, "Who remembers the Armenians?" Growing up in Skokie, Illinois, a predominantly Jewish suburb, I came to understand how the Holocaust left an indellible mark on the collective Jewish psyche, much like the Genocide for the Armenians and slavery for African Americans. I would like to educate myself on the historical facts surrounding the Holocaust by defining the key terminology associated with the Holocaust and providing a historical overview of this period. Amidst revisionist writers who claim that the Holocaust never happened, the need to learn the historical truth and pass it down is ever more important. Thus, this paper will provide a historical road map of the Holocaust and the experiences of those who endured and surived.

Why Study the Holocaust?

The Holocaust was a watershed event which was historically unprecedented in scope. It reveals the disastrous consequences of prejudice, racism, and social and political scapegoating that are endemic in any society. The German example also shows that democratic institutions are not permanent political fixtures. Rather, they can be overturned, as did occur in Germany, if citizens are not politically aware. By studying the Holocaust and the horrific effects this historical event had on a certain contingent of society, we can gain understanding of past history in hopes of preventing the repetition of it.

Who were the Nazis?

"Nazi" is a short term for the National Socialist German Workers Party. This was a right-wing political party originally established in 1919 primarily by unemployed German veterans of World War I. In January 1933, Hindenburg appointed Hitler chancellor of a coalition cabinet. Hitler, who took office on January 30, 1933, immediately set up a dictatorship. In 1934, the chancellorship and presidency were united in the person of the Fuhrer. The Nazi party ideology was strongly anti-Communist, antisemitic, racist, nationalistic, imperialistic and militaristic. During this totalitarian period, all other parties were outlawed and opposition was brutally suppressed. He ended German democracy and severely restricted basic rights such as freedom of personal and marital relations, freedom of speech, press and assembly. The Nazis even intervened in the bedroom and prohibited inter-racial marriage and sexual relations! Hitler and the Nazis succeeded in establishing a brutal dictatorship that defined people by race and punished dissidents through a vicious reign of terror. This created an atmosphere of fear, distrust and suspicion in which people betrayed their neighbors. This all helped the Nazis to obtain control over social institutions such as the civil service, the educational system, churches, the judiciary, industry, business and other professions.

What were the concentration camps?

Immediately upon their assumption of power on January 30, 1933, the Nazis established concentration campus for the imprisonment of all "enemies" of their regime: actual and potential political opponents including communists, socialists, bolsheviks, gypsies, homosexuals, and other asocials. Beginning in 1938, Jews were targeted for internment solely because they were Jews. Before then, only Jews that fit one of the earlier categories were interned in campus. The first three concentration campus established were Dachau (near Munich), Buchenwald (near Weimar) and Sachsenhausen (near Berlin). Auschwitz became the most notorious of concentration camps. It was both a labor and extermination camp in upper Silesia, Poland, 37 miles west of Krakow. Established in 1940 as a concentration camp, it became an extermination center in 1942. Forced labor, medical experimentation, along with extermination were the goals of the camps. The Auschwitz extermination procedure evolved in stages. "In April 1942, Slovak Jews were gassed in Crematorium I, apparently with their clothes on. Later, deportees from nearby Sosnowiec were told to undress in the yard. The victims, faced by the peremptory order to remove their clothes, men in front of women and women in front of men, became apprehensive. The SS men, shouting at them, then drove the naked men, women and children into the gas chamber"(click here for reference Auschwitz).

Historical Overview

 The first measures taken by the Nazis against the Jews began in 1933. On April 1 of this year, the Nazis organized a boycott of Jewish shops and businesses by the Nazis. Later that week, the law for the Re-establishment of the Civil Service expelled all non-Aryans (defined as anyone with a Jewish parent or grandparent) from the civil service. Initially, exceptions were made for those working since August 1914, German veterans of WWI, and those who had lost a father or son fighting for Germany or her allies in WWI. Later on in the same year, Jews were prohibited from becoming practicing lawyers, physicians, civil servants, and academics. Jewish enrollment in German high schools also was limited to 1.5% of the student body. Jews were forced into creating their own schools and subculture away from mainstream Germany in order to survive. As mentioned earlier, the events of the Holocaust can be categorized historically in two main phases.
  1. From 1933-1939, the politics of daily German life took a distinctly anti-Jewish slant. During this time period, intermittent attacks on invidual Jewish businesses and people, as well as the institutionalization of racism took place. Beginning in 1933, laws were put into place that essentially excluded Jews from the economy. They could no longer work in a vast array of professions and could not own their own businesses in many districts. Thus, the Nazis succeeded their legal channels of slowly marginalizing the Jewish community and disarming them of their property, money, and influence in society.
  2. From 1939-1945 instituted the second phase of the Holocaust. On September 1, 1939, Germany invaded Poland and World War II began. Within days, the Polish army was defeated and the Nazis began their campaign to destroy those non-Aryans whom they viewed as "subhuman." This second phase was characterized by the same racist ideology as the earlier period with one main difference. After 1939, the racism and intermittent harassment of Jews became more serious and bureaucratized. No longer were just a few Jews that the SS found displeasing harassed, but the whole Jewish population in Germany. Ghettos were set up in order that Germans could "relocate" Jews in their own quarters within Germany. During this period, camps were set up and 6 million Jews ultimately killed. The war ended in 1945 and brought some, but not all, Nazi war criminals to justice through the Nuremberg Trials.
