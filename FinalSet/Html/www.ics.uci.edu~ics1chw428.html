Introduction

The Middle East is a region that has, especially in recent years, become the focus of the world's attention. In this assignment, we are going to focus on three regions in particular: Jordan,Israel, and Palestine. The problem with attributing a geographical location to a "Palestinian" state is that there is officially not such a state in existence (yet). Instead, Palestinians live on a majority of the land in the Gaza Strip and the West Bank of the Jordan River. In particular, we will pay special notice to some of the important political and economic aspects of each country/region, with a special focus on the regions' constitutions, modern historical conflict, prospects for peace, and economics. Although religion obviously plays some role in the conflict, we have chosen to zero in on the purely political and economic concerns of the region.

The Political Landscapes of Jordan, Israel, and the Palestinians

Jordan and the Constitution

Jordan, or The Hashemite Kingdom of Jordan (named after the Hashemite ruling family) has a Constitution which, among other things, serves as the "framework" for the state. In particular, Jordan's constitution contains nine components. Like the American constitution, Jordan's constitution contains provisions for an executive power (which discusses the process by which the monarchy proceeds), the national assembly (comprised of both a senate and chamber of deputies), and the judiciary (which, interestingly enough, is divided into courts that deal specifically with civil, religious, and special matters). In addition, Jordan's constitution provides for certain duties and freedoms of Jordan's citizens. Such freedoms include land rights, personal freedoms, and labor law.

Israel's Political Structure

Israel's Political Structure parallels that found in many European countries (because it is based on a parliamentary system). It is comprised of three main branches, the Israeli Legislature (a.k.a., the Knesset), the Israeli Executive Branch, and the Israeli Judiciary. Israel does not have an official "constitution" per se, but does have a number of Basic Laws adopted by the Knesset that seem to serve a similar purpose. The Basic Laws represent the Law of the Land in Israel. In particular, the basic laws have helped establish the Knesset, the President, the Government, the State Economy, the Israel Defense Forces (a.k.a. "the IDF"), the Judiciary, and the office of the State Comptroller. In addition, the Basic Laws have included special measures on state lands, the city of Jerusalem, Human Dignity and Liberty, and Freedom of Occupation.

The Political Structure of the Palestinian People

As stated earlier on, the Palestinian people are not in possession of an official constitution as of yet, mostly because there is no "official" Palestinian state. However, Palestinians have considered themselves a distinct people whose homeland was taken from them. As a result, Palestinians have produced numerous documents that serve as a means of establishing both a national identity and a means of self-governance (even under occupation). For example, the Palestinians have legitimized much of their struggle on the Palestinian Right of Return. The Right of Return is based on, in large part, to the document that comes closest to a Palestinian constitution--the Palestinian National Charter. The Charter was created between July 1-17, 1968, and comprises thirty-three articles. Each article defined what a "Palestinian" was (when the document was first created ), as well as providing the rationale for their claim to the land. However, parts of the charter have been altered as a result of the 1993 peace agreement with Israel--paving the way for a future Palestinian state in the West Bank and Gaza.

An Overview-The History of The Arab-Israeli Conflict

After years of war following its independence, Israel has come to partial terms with Jordan and the Palestinians. Before we go into the current peace agreements, however, it's important to put the conflict between the three sides into context.

Following its inception, Israel was involved in the 1956 Sinai War. Israel, in its own view, felt that it was being threatened by the nations of Egypt, Syria, and Jordan (all three of which had formed a military alliance). The debate over whether Israel actually was threatened has been a matter of debate. Nevertheless, it carried out an attack against Egypt's Sinai Peninsula (along with France and Great Britain). After much coaxing from the United States, Israel agreed to withdraw its troops and turn over the Suez Canal.

The second main war Israel was involved in is also a point of controversy, depending on which side you talk to. Israel's View of the 6-Day War is that it was justified in launching an attack on Egypt, Syria, and Jordan once again because it felt these countries were massing troops for an offensive attack of their own. The Arab View of the 1967 War claims that Israel was the instigator. Egypt, Syria, and Jordan say Israel knew full well that the Arabs had no intention of attacking. Whatever one's view, Israel ended up destroying much of the air and land forces of the opposing countries. As a result of the 1967 War, Israel captured the Sinai Peninsula and Gaza Strip from Egypt, the Golan Heights from Syria, and the West Bank of the Jordan River from Jordan. It was after the war that the Palestine Liberation Organization was formed. In Israel's eyes, the PLO was a terrorist group bent on destruction. However, the PLO (and most of the Arab world, for that matter) viewed itself as conducting a legitimate struggle to free the Palestinians living in the West Bank and Gaza Strip from Israeli occupation.

The next conflict between Israel and these countries was the 1973 Yom Kippur War. This conflict began when both Egypt and Syria (Jordan was not involved) launched an attack on Israel. Only with military aid from the United States was Israel able to repel the attack. Shortly after, the sides signed an agreement, in which Israel withdrew from parts of the Sinai Peninsula and the Golan Heights.

The last major war Israel was involved in was code-named Operation Peace for Galilee. This 1982 conflict resulted after the PLO was forced to move its headquarters into the south part of Lebanon. The PLO continuously launched attacks into Israel, and Israel responded by attacking back. However, Israel's attack proved to be devastating in loss of life for the PLO, Palestinian refugees, Lebanese civilians, and the Israeli military itself. To this day, Israel maintains a "security zone" in the south part of Lebanon (which encompasses about ten percent of the country's total land area).

A Chance for Peace?

Following the 1991 Persian Gulf War, the United States and the former Soviet Union pressured Israel, Jordan, Syria, and the Palestinians to the bargaining table to negotiate a final peace settlement. Following the 1992 Israeli elections, a more moderate government under Yitzhak Rabin came into power. In the summer of 1993, a major breakthrough occurred. Israel and the Palestinians agreed, for the first time on both sides, to recognize each other. This Israel-PLO recognition resulted in a Declaration of Principles (which was signed on the White House Lawn). In addition to the recognition, Israel agreed to give Palestinians the power of self-rule in Gaza and the West Bank town of Jericho (with Israeli troop removal from these immediate areas and the remainder of the West Bank in the future).

Following this treaty, Jordan and Israel experienced a breakthrough in their negotiations. They ended up signing a treaty of their own. The 1994 Jordan-Israel Peace Treaty contains thirty articles, with provisions on (among other things)water, economic relations, and energy. This treaty was also signed at the White House, and officially ended forty-six years of war between the two sides.

The Future of Peace

What the future holds for these three parties is somewhat uncertain. The Israel-Jordan agreement, in our own view, will remain in place (as long as both governments remain stable). The Palestinian-Israeli agreement, however, might be in jeopardy. Many (but not most) Jews and Arabs remain against the peace process, including the group HAMAS. A big sticking point for the next few years is the status of Israel's capital of Jerusalem. Both Palestinians and Israelis claim the city as their own. The fact remains, however, that Palestinians live in the East sector of the city, while Israelis live in the West sector of the city. Palestinians want the East to become the eventual capital of a Palestinian state, but Israel does not want to see the city divided. To give a background on how divided the city has been through the years, you can look at the history of Jerusalem. The city began between the years 1000 and 961 B.C., when King David reigned. Since then, the city has gone through the conquest of the Persian Empire, the Hasmoneans (or the Maccabees), the Romans, the Byzantines, the Muslims, the European crusaders, the Mamelukes, the Ottomans, the British, and Israel today. Even in our present time, Jerusalem is considered holy to three religions (Islam, Judaism, and Christianity).

The next main issue that has concerned both sides is the rate of Israeli troop withdrawl from the rest of the West Bank. Palestinians want to see a faster troop withdrawl, whereas Israel contends the troops will stay until the PLO can stop terrorist attacks. A related issue is the demand that Israel re-instate Palestinians expelled from the West Bank and Gaza Strip.

A third sticking point is the question over water rights. For whatever reason, this issue was not included in the Declaration of Principles. Some, like Dr. Nabil M. El-Khodary believe the omission of the water issue was a political move by Israel and was therefore intentional. Again, as with many issues in the Middle East, everyone involved has their opinion. No matter how one slices it, though, it will remain contentious until a possible settlement.

The Economies of Jordan, Israel, and the Palestinians

As we have seen, certain parts of the peace treaties between Jordan, Israel, and the Palestinians are dependent upon healthy economic relations. In this section of the assignment, we'll describe what the economic picture of each area looks like--how wealthy (or how poor) the country/area is, what each area is dependent upon for economic sustainability, and other related statistics.

Jordan

Jordan's economy is very dependent on the economic health of the other countries. In particular, Jordan relies on financial support from the Gulf States' oil rich monarchies.(lsaid@netcom.com) The " physical resource bases of Jordan are extremely limited, which precludes any substantial development of primary economic activity(even if the constraints imposed by the size of their domestic markets could be overcome."(lsaid@netcom.com) We believe that Jordan has relied more on its human resources (which are plentiful) rather than its physical ones.

The Palestinians

Palestine (i.e., the land commonly known as the West Bank and Gaza Strip) is primarily used for agricultural purposes. It contains "10,162 square mile(27,000 square kilometres) of land plus 272 square miles of inland water."(Mohammed Abu Sharikh) According to Mr. Mohammed Abu Sharikh, the human resource base of the Palestinian population is estimated to be 6,409,036. The Palestinian people are dependent on agriculture. Because of the Mediterranean climate, they can grow a wide assortment of crops year-round. These crops are, in turn, sent out to Europe and other Gulf countries.

Israel

Only one economy in the Middle East can really be compared to those found in Europe--that of Israel. Living standards are high, and compare favorably with European levels. "The country boasts a highly diversified economy It not only produces goods for immediate consumption, but also uses components in local assembly industries and... machinery."(Yigal Caspi). The growth of Israel's economy is very high compared to other nations in the Middle East. "Between 1948 and 1958 high immigration accelerated development in order to achieve a reasonable standard of living. Then from 1960 to 1972 annual growth was 9.2%."(Yigal Caspi) Israel's economy does not depend upon agriculture or industry; however, they are shifting to a high-tech industrial economy.

Conclusion

So concludes our presentation on the areas of Jordan, Israel, and Palestine. The constitutional/governmental structures of each place have especially been points of interest (to us, at least). In addition, they demonstrate how these areas govern themselves. The history of the Arab-Israeli conflict has had important effects on the three countries covered in this assignment. After years of war, each side has hammered out a peace agreement: hopefully paving the way for a lasting peace. The economic characteristics of Jordan, Israel, and the Palestinians are important because they may very well play a role in future negotiations and peace. An economic area in particular that has already proven to be a point of debate is water resources. This will have to be one of many issues negotiated on in the coming years. In our opinion, peace can be achieved--it's just a matter of how it's achieved.
