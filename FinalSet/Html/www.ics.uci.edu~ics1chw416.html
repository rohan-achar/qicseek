"LAY OFF THE NET": PRIVACY AND CENSORSHIP ON THE INTERNET
by Steve Hoffman<easu568@ea.oac.uci.edu>

I. INTRODUCTION

The arrival of massive on-line computer networks of information exchange such as the Internet has given rise to a whole range of unique issues and controversies related to privacy, censorship, and the law. For instance, difficulties in maintaining privacy has led to a restructuring in the means by which governmental bodies collect data on their citizens, one of the most notable cases perpetuated by the FBI. This in turn has generated concern and resistance over the extent in which government surveillance and intrusion on the Internet is possible and/or advisable. Also, while attempts to censor have tended to center around topics related to sex, pornography, and sexuality (which are all subjects which classically fall under the legal guise of "obscene"), the implementation of censorship and regulatory laws governing content could conceivably spread to the extent that they jeopardize free speech and access across the board. Simultaneously, the Internet has proven an especially difficult and/or problematic format on which to implement censorship laws. While computer networks have managed to remain relatively open and resistant to attempts at regulation, the drastic increase in the use of such information retrieving, accessing, and relaying tools such as the World Wide Web virtually ensures that these issues will increase in the forseeable future(not to mention the issues related to the Web's use of detailed graphics and images).

The on-line community certainly has not reacted passively to attempts at regulation and surveillance. Groups, such as the Electronic Frontier Foundation and the Computers and Academic Freedom Archive, have popped up to monitor attempts at regulation, taking a decidedly "civil liberties" stance against any form of infringements upon open and free speech. In addition, newly emergent issues never cease to pop up, including the current bill in the US Congress (FBI's history of covert intelligence activities would lay testament to this). Thus the question becomes, to what extent should governmental access to personal information on the Internet be allowed? What measures/precations need be taken to deter abusive governmental intrusion of the on-line community?

The increasing computerization of public records and databases adds an entirely new element to these debates. For example, the ever advanced technology available to the public also implies that government bodies have ever advancing ways of collecting data and covert means of accessing it. One example is the proposals for "smart highways" which enable the constant tracking of all automobiles on the road. Given examples like these, concern over surveillance are fairly obvious.

Laws regarding privacy and surveillance on computerized networks such as the Internet vary extensively across states and among service providers. There have been some fairly limited attempts at passing federal laws which protect personal information on computer networks, including the Privacy Act of 1974, but these are outdated and lack in uniform clarity as to percisely what they protect. Additionally, current law is not tailored to the unique dynamics involved in the massive informational interchange inherent to a system like the Internet. Possibly the most evident concern is with the use of electronic mail devises and on-line chat services such as IRC. Anonymity in such exchanges is far from assured, and current law makes little provisions toward thwarting vulnerability to surveillance.

Rather than curb surveillance capabilities, a powerful attempt recently arose that aimed at greatly abating government surveillance. In 1992 the FBI attempted to implement the "Digital Telephony" legislation. This was a response by the FBI and other law enforcement agencies to a concern that changes in telecommunications technology would jeopardize their ability to conduct "authorized wiretapping". The proposal mandated that all providers of electronic communications services provide law enforcement officials the "capability and capacity" to survey and intercept on-line transmissions, without the detection of the user. This is essentially an attempt to modernize the wiretap statues passed in 1968 and 1986 by Congress. The danger in such proposals is in instilling arcane monitoring techniques to the dynamically open and constantly evolving Internet. In addition to its oppressively broad scope, the legislation had provisions that would have allowed law enforcement agencies to force providers to use certain technologies that are of most use to their data collecting purposes. In this sense then, the law would go far beyond the provisions set out by the wiretap laws already in existence by actually requiring providers to adopt a certain standard of tools. The wire-tap bill unleashed a torrent of responses within the on-line community, as many objected to the negative ramifications such a broadly scoped surveillance bill would inflict. Nevertheless, on October 7, 1994, the bill finally passed Congress, but in a much modified form. The final draft, entitled the Edwards/Leahy Digital Telephony Legislation does place functional design requirements on providers but does not allow for heightened authority to conduct wiretapping operations.

III. CENSORSHIP, ACCESS, AND THE LAW

Whereas the concern for the on-line community regarding privacy issues has revolved around protection from undo governmental intrusion, a related though distinct issue of free and open access to informational services has surrounded the issue of censorship on the Internet. The Internet has proven particularly resistant to attempts at regulating its contents, due in part to the "ethos" of its users who tend to regard free and open access at upmost importance(an interesting editorial on limiting Internet freedom addresses this point at length). Probably more telling however is the difficulty in actually banning materials from it. The Internet is one of the most easily accessible and exploitable information forums in existence. The advent of the World Wide Web makes putting information on the Net all the easier, and thus all the more resistant to attempts at regulation.

Probably the most recurrent issue related to censorship both on the Net and in general is topics involving sex. One of the most controversial of the sex related sources on the Internet is a newsgroup named "alt.sex". This newsgroup carries an extensive array of erotic essays, sexually explicit stories, sex related discussion groups, and issues of sexuality in general. By far the most targeted for censorship out of these is stories which describe in often lurid and graphic detail tales of sexual violence, degradation of women, and even explicit depictions of rape and other forms of forced and/or coerced sex. To exasperate the tension arisen by such material even further, the World Wide Web's multimedia capabilities allows users to pull up pornographic pictures in full color. These means that virtually anyone old enough to understand how to operate a Web browser can have access to such material. It quickly becomes apparent that the safeguards to exposure to pornographic material available at a store (age of purchase laws) are no longer applicable in this new format.

In addition, concern has increased over a number of issues relating to various debates over the distinction between the terms "offensive and harrassment". Generally, merely offensive material is protected under free speech, whereas harrassment constitutes a hate crime punishable by law. Where one draws the line between these two is a constant source of ambiguity. Likewise, debate over what falls under such laws as the UW Rule banning discriminatory speech (or "hate speech") from academic settings have been increased. For example, should University officials take punitive action upon repeatedly racist jokes and/or stories over their computer networks? Given that practically anyone can post anything they want on the Net, (thereby eliminating safeguards on validity of claims, speech codes, etc.) should there be greater attention paid to limiting access and/or ban certain types of transmissions above and beyond current censorship law?

So far, policies regarding "suppression and expression" vary among service providers, but in all are fairly limited. The hesitance at implementing regulatory laws to govern network transmission is reflective of the difficulty in both deciding what constitutes material to be censored and how to go about it in an environment such as the Internet. Attempts at controlling transmissions have been fairly unsuccessful, and come at the risk of academic openness and the free access so many Net users have come to value. This is not to say censorship is unheard of on the Internet, as a quick look at a document which lists banned materials in 1992 at major academic institutions bears out.

Possibly the most comprehensive attempt to impose a regulatory measure on material on the Internet is a current proposal under consideration in the U.S. Congress, the Senate Bill S. 314. This bill, introduced by Senators Exon and Gorton, attempts to impose current FCC regulations(which govern radio and television programming and content) regarding "obscene and indecent" material to all networked electronic communication outlets. Entitled the "Communications Decency Act of 1995", if passed the act would incur criminal penalty to providers who allowed such material to appear on their network. Thus, the bill would require service providers to screen nearly all of the transmissions occurring on their networks in order to root out any possible "offenders". In an analysis by the Center for Democracy and Technology, the authors point out that "service providers would be forced to closely monitor every private communication, electronic mail message, public forum, mailing list, and file archive carried by or available on their network, a proposition which poses a substantial threat to the freedom of speech and privacy rights of all American citizens".

What becomes interesting here is the collusion of censorship issues with surveillance/privacy issues. Should S. 314 pass, not only would it entail the censorship of material which fell under the rubric of the 1934 Communications Act(specifically, Section 223, that prevents minors access to "indecent" audiotext), it would also force service providers to constantly monitor the types of transmissions occurring at all times. In effect, the bill seems to be another example of a broadly scoped and poorly drafted attempt too apply out-dated methods of surveillance and regulation to technology designed in large part to counteract such restrictionists. The Internet relys on the speedy and open transfer of information. Given the drain already on providers just to offer basic access, a bill such as S. 314would unduly afflict both provider and user with impractical guidelines and regulations.

IV. ORGANIZATIONS DEALING WITH THESE ISSUES

Numerous organizations have popped up since the Internet's inception to monitor and advocate positions regarding issues of privacy and censorship on computer networks. Two of the most prominent of these groups are the Electronic Frontier Foundation(EFF) and Computers and Academic Freedom(CAF). The EFF deals especially with issues involving Constitutional rights of the First Amendment of the Bill of Rights, and "democratic values". Their stated goals include establishing new laws, policies for network providers, a "national information infrastructure", and providing a format in which "all citizens" have input in the "information age". They also sponsor legal cases that involve violations of users' civil liberties and maintain useful archives of legislation, cases, and incidences of censorship. The group also puts out position papers on related issues and impending legislation that effects the on-line community.

Computers and Academic Freedom provide an incredibly extensive array of papers dealing in issues of privacy, access, and censorship, as well as maintaining vast archives of case work. Their FAQ page is an excellent quick reference to some of the major debates and questions regarding telecommunication regulation.

V. CONCLUSION

In no way should this brief overview of issues of censorship and privacy be construed as exhaustive. Literally thousands of individual cases have sprung up. This paper has attempted to deal with some of the major trends regarding these topics, some of the overlying concerns for the on-line community, current issues and bills that threaten to alter how the Internet operates, and lastly, two of the best sources to go to in order to keep abreast on any crucial developments. Privacy and censorship on the Net will undoubtedly remain prevalent and hotly contested in the future. As long as one remains an engaged user of the Net, one should not fail to stay informed.
