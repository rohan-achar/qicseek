The Future, The Past And The Present

Strolling down the halls of a museum one can hear the echoes of a culture shouting their existence in the form of paint, metal, plaster, glass and silicon. Silicon? Yes folks it was bound to happen sooner than later. Today man has decided to store his a== rt not on canvas but disk. Welcome to the future of art in the 21st century. Looking ahead we have seen our magazines, books, music and pictures that define the human existence go digital. In fact almost every media imaginable has now gone the ways of the cyber-community. Gone are our the conventions that would limit our access to art, science and government. People worldwide now gather across phone lines and push buttons that inject the new messia h. A digital homage to a Mecca that is continually growing upward to an infinite sky. This new genre has entered our society with a vehemence of unwreakening strength. Man in the last four years has invented another Atomic Bomb, and it's buttons lie wi th in Browsers such as Mosaic and Netscape. This time we are all under attack.

Art has been the one true universal element in all human societies. With the Egyptians it was a way of recording events and honoring the memories of the dead. With the Greeks and Romans art became a decorative element that enhanced daily life. In Africa as in many tribal communities art was a way to worship gods and deities. In fac== t since the dawn of man, art expressed mans creative desire and depiction for beauty and tranquillity. Much is the same with our modern society as it was millions of years ago. The difference now being the format not the purpose.

With the advent of the World Wide Web, art is expressed though in-line images of a Browser, stored in universal compression formats such as GIF, JPEG and MPEG. It is here that a new definition is being mapped out for art and its role in a computer based society. In this new art world everyone who has access can be seen while new i deas are inputted and updated constantly. I first became aware of this new cyberbased art world one year ago when I ran across the Otis Project on the Web. It was here that the interactive nature of computing was being retrofitt ed into an art context. I quickly took the opportunity to take some scanned photos and paintings I had worked on, and then uploaded them to the site. It was through my newly acquired audience reactions that I developed an interest in the potential of digital art. It was then that I dove into the ideas surrounding art on the net. Quickly, I discovered that I was now an information provider. This newly acquired title forced me to think of the social responsibility as well as the implications that the new technology has upon society. In this new age everyone involved is an artist and critic. The range of images and information is unfathomable. Navigating to w== eb site museums had become a long time consuming quest for art. Luckily for artists search engines cut most of the time required to find art based Web sites. Yahoo is a good starting location for hunting down Web Galleries. It's here that one can find a listing of many commercial galleries as well as non-profit galleries. Most Universities have now joined the Web bringing student work (that might be limited to a particular campus) on-line.

Still, if this new technology was to have any implications in revolutionizing the existing art world, it would need to reference it. The Smithsonian Institute site is one that exemplifies how an old establishment is willin g to modernize. If one was interested in viewing art of the impressionists or the Great Masters a trip to the Luovre no longer requires getting on a pla== ne to Europe.

Unfortunately the Web has its limitations. It can never replace the original work nor can it represent the environment on feels when in a gallery or museum. What the Web lacks it makes up for with convenience. In most cases a gallery visitor might not have time or money to venture to visit a friends show. With a Web gallery one can still visit and see their friends work in this case lets say Greg Bowen from the Illinois State University. By clicking the hypertext document I just saved myself a trip across the country to view pictures of his girlfriends navel piercing.

The real test of this computer based renaissance will be in research. This field offers archeologists as well as art historians to a myriad of files and images stored on the net. Commercial implications aside. This type of research might replace the th ousands of journals in the libraries across the world. For example. A subject calls for a study on Egyptian art during the Middle Kingdom period. One only needs to access a site in Memphis. In fact the longer one pursues the subject art and artifacts from around the world can be found and brought into the home via the WWW. Art enters our computer driven society though other sources besides the WWW. In the form of CD-ROM, hundreds of images can be placed on Compact Disk. This type of packaging unfortunately requires a certain type of platform in order to run, are not as interactive as the WWW (also they can be very expensive). I have found that the WWW is the only viable solution to the computer driven art community. Low costs and a never ending data bank of information will propel the 21st century artist into the future. Unfortunately the ride will not be smooth.

Until recently the images found on the internet went unchallenged. Art no matter what its content could be distributed world wide. In some essence it was the last place an artist could express his/her ideas with out censorship imposed upon their work. Now, as in most anarchy's, the art and images found on the net are being challenged by government on its content. The issues of pornography vs. artistic vision will again be brought into the political areana, as the numbers of WWW traffic increases. A nude study such as Hisayoshi Osawa's might be compared to images found on Playboy Internet. I believe if restrictions are placed upon art, it cannot exist in its true virtue. It is a difficult issue to approach and has no real solution except to educated the viewers in the differences in the meanings. Judging from experience Art has always challenged censo== r ideals and has prevailed, even in a conservative society.

What the future holds in the art world is still being shaped. It is quite possible that this technology is a trend and it will pass with the new and improved technology. I feel some of the concepts mentioned above are to stay. Twenty years ago a digita== l media based society was unimaginable to the public, today it is inconceivable to live without it. From our televisions to our laptops, we rely on the information we obtain. Art is connected to this information society, from it we get the pictures and the sounds that shape cultures. Today the world is united in this new kingdom of silicon. It is here that art can truly progress into a wonderful arena of ideas that can be viewed by all. The WWW is the key that, for now, unlocks the doors to the world's family album. Sven Wechsler a student from the University of Colorado commented on the value of art on the Internet. He jests "The Louvre isn't exactly across the street." Today it is.
