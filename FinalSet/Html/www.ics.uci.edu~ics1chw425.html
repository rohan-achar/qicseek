A General Overview of Who Is an Orthodox Christian:


An Orthodox Christian takes her or his beliefs from two sources: "the Scriptures and [the] Sacred Tradition" (The Orthodox Christian). The Orthodox Christian belongs to the body of Christ, which through time, has transformed into the "Church of Christ" (The Orthodox Christian). In Orthodoxy, this Church of Christ is essentially the accumulation of descendants from the original congregation "which was born at the outpouring of the Holy Spirit in Jerusalem on Pentecost" (The Orthodox Christian). This first congregation was called an ecclesia. The Apostles were the first priests, who then passed on the position and tradition to the priests of the oncoming generations.

The Bible in Relation to Holy Tradition in the Orthodox Faith


In Orthodoxy, the Bible in only part of the richness of Christian tradition. Although Orthodox Christians turn to the Bible for wisdom and guidance, they do not resort only on the Bible for information on how to live. The Bible, or Holy Scripture, is only part of the Holy Tradition. For Orthodox Christians, while the Holy Scripture is a "beautiful gem" (The Bible and Holy Tradition) and a central part of Orthodoxy, it's power will only manifest itself when one places it in the context of the larger Holy Tradition.

History of the Orthodox Church


The Orthodox church was founded by Jesus Christ and His disciples; the faith and rituals continue to grow now for twenty centuries. Although Orthodox Christians exist in other cultures and countries as well, most of them are "Greek, Russian, Egyptian, Romanian, Serbian, Ukrainian, Armenian, Bulgarian, Albanian, Ethiopian, [or] Syrian" (The Orthodox Christian Fellowship Homepage) in their ethnicity.

Orthodox believers adhere to the Nicene Creed, which is the most widely accepted statement used in the Christian faith. It is a "Common Ground" (The Nicence Creed) to the Eastern Orthodox, Roman Catholic, Anglican, Lutheran, Calvinist, and other Christian churches. The creed was created to support strongly the fact that "Jesus was fully God" (The Nicene Creed). In other words, He was a part of the one God (Father, Son and Holy Spirit) and not just a creation that God made.

Some Differences Between the Orthodox Church and the Catholic and Protestant Churches


The Orthodox and the Protestant Churches
One of the main differences between the Orthodox and the Protestant Church lies precisely in the varying weight they place on the Holy Scriptures. Both churches adhere to the centrality of the Bible in their faiths. However, for the Protestant, the Bible is the only source of guidance, while for the Orthodox, the Bible is only part of the Holy Tradition. The Orthodox doctrine finds a key problem with the Protestant stance: "gradually all of Holy Tradition has been stripped away either because someone didn't like this or that piece or perhaps the true beauty of a portion was tarnished and it was tossed away without knowing its true value or perhaps a cheap substitute attempting to replace that which was lost was done away with etc. until all that remains of the Tradition of the Church is the Bible," writes Father David Moser.

The Orthodox and the Catholic Churches
The Orthodox Church differs from the Catholic Church as well: about 500 years before the reformation split western Europe into Protestants and Roman Catholics, the Orthodox protested against the Pope and his authority. The Orthodox had two main reasons for refusing the Pope. Firstly, they did not agree with his attempts to become "supreme over the church" ( Some Straight Answers about the Orthodox Church) in the 11th century. Secondly, they were against the fact that the Pope proposed "some doctrinal innovations" ( Some Straight Answers about the Orthodox Church). Because the Church until that point had remained "unchanged in doctrine and faith since the early Church of the Apostles" ( Some Straight Answers about the Orthodox Church ), the Orthodox rejected the changes of the Pope (and thus, of the Roman Catholics). The Orthodox Church does consider itself "Catholic," however, in another sense; it is "whole and not confined" (Some Straight Answers about the Orthodox Church) in nature.

The Coptic Orthodox Church


A Look Into the Coptic Orthodox Church


As previously mentioned, Orthodox Christians are prevalent in several different cultures. Although they are virtually the same in doctrine and practice, it will be useful to focus on a particular segment in more detail. I will focus on the Coptic Orthodox Church of Egypt because it offers the most information in the internet.

The Coptic Orthodox Church is a "very conservative Church, which preserves most carefully the Christian Faith, in its earliest and purest form, passed on from generation to generation, unaltered and true to the Apostolic doctrine and patterns of worship" (Copts Through the Ages). Furthermore, it is a "deeply spiritual and even mystical Church, with an emphasis upon holiness, and the Mysteries of Faith, but at the same time it is a strongly doctrinal Church" (Copts Through the Ages) that not only adheres to the Scripture, but also to the teachings of the holy Apostles.

The word Copt derives from the word "Aigyptos," a Greek word which refers to the first capitol of Ancient Egypt. However, the modern use of the word Coptic refers to the Egyptian Orthodox Christians. The Coptic Church is based on Saint Mark's teachings. The religion is very old, for "Saint Mark...brought Christianity to Egypt" ( The Christian Coptic Orthodox Church of Egypt) during the first centuty, only a dozen years after Jesus ascended into heaven. The Coptic religion is authentic in that it dates back almost to the beginning of the Orthodox Church, but also in that some have discovered part of Saint John's gospel written in the Coptic language.

The Coptic Church's Contributions to the Orthodox Faith


The Copts have made numerous contributions to the Orthodox faith as a whole. Saint Mark was one of the first contributers, for he "had opened the first Christian Catechetical School in Alexandria for the instruction of the new converts" (Copts Through the Ages). Other Coptic saints made lasting contributions as well. For instance, Saint Athanasius, a Copt and the Pope of Alexandria in 381 A.D., authored the above mentioned Nicene Creed. Furthermore, monasticism grew out of Egypt, and subscribed to the character of "humbleness and submission" (Nicene Creed). Later, Saint Anthony, a Copt, was the first Christian monk during the last years of the third century. Also a Copt, Saint Pachom later established the rules of monasticism.
