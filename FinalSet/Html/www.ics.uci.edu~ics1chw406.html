Current Techniques For Earthquake Prediction

Introduction

	Earthquakes are one of our oldest enemies, but it has not
been until recently that we have been able to understand what they
are and how they are caused.  One of the reasons why they are so
feared is they because of their apparent randomness.  Earthquakes
are powerful, but more intimidating is their invisibility, for
they seem to appear out of nowhere.  Ironically, earthquakes are
only disasterous if they have struck a populated area.  By
themselves, they are nature's what of releasing built up energy.
Data collected at the U.S. Geological Survey Center
states that an earthquake with a onemagnitude change creates 10 times
more ground motion and releases 32 times more energy. One of the obvious
methods to reduce the loss of life and injury caused by earthquakes is to 
be able to predict the earthquake before it arrives.  In this research 
paper, I will attempt to outline some of the long-term, 
short-term, and instantaneous earthquake prediction techniques.

Long-Term Prediction Techniques

	The long-term goals of earthquake prediction allows us to
determine the possiblity of an earthquake years before it strikes. 
However, earthquakes are extremely large-scale phenomenas that exist in
3-D space within the earth's mantle.
	This makes it exceedingly difficult to predict in the long-run. 
Fortunately, however, earthquakes do have patterns, and these patterns are
what increases our chances of a successful prediction.
  • Statistical Return Periods

    One way to help predict earthquakes is through statistical return periods. For example, the Department of Geophysics at Caltech keeps track of all earthquakes in California, whether they are minor or major. By studying earthquake reoccurence at the same approximate location over a long period of time, we might be able to reasonably predict when another earthquake will occur. For this method to be effective, we must have a long and accurate historical record.

  • Earthquake Characteristics

    In addition to studying the frequency of certain earthquakes, scientists can also study its characteristics. Seismologists Robert S. Crosson and Stephen D. Malone at the Department of Geophysics at the University of Washington work in conjunction with the Pacific Northwest Seismograph Network to help determine the structure of earthquakes. The PNSN is a collection of more than a hundred various seismic stations surrounding the Pacific Northwest. Armed with sophisticated equipment, seismologists can retreive real-time detection data from an occuring earthquake. By collecting data from a broad area during an earthquake, we can learn more about its motional characteristics. These motional characteristics may, in the future, help us to predict when and where an earthquake will appear.

Short-Term Prediction Techniques

	While long-term predictions are on the order of years and decades,
short-term predictions are on the order of days and possibly, hours.
  • Precursory Phenomena

    One way to predict an earthquake is through precursory phenomena. Examples include dropping water levels in wells, peculiar animal behavior, and even strange glows in the sky. While there are no scientific explanations for these occurences, very high stresses in rocks produce heat, cause deformation of rocks, releases gases, and other geomagnetic effects. It is highly possible that these effects can alternate the normal surface of the earth.

  • Foreshocks

    Another possible indicator of a upcoming earthquake is the occurence of foreshocks, or small earthquakes before a main shock. In the Questions and Answer On Earthquakes at the University of Nevada, Reno Seismological Laboratory, the chance of another earthquake the same size or larger than the previous quake is 6%. As time passes, that percentage drops. This method od prediction lies on the fact that the earth's core releases pressure in small packets before the main rupture. A single earthquake has a low probability of leading to a larger quake, but several small earthquakes in an area identified as a seismic gap can increase the probability.

Instantaneous Prediction Techniques

  • Alarm Systems

    Scientists have also been able to predict an earthquake within seconds of its occurence. Unfortunately, the only sure way to predict an earthquake is after it has happened. Fortunately, however, in a number of cases, the danger of an earthquake comes from the shock waves arriving from an earthquake with its epicenter some distance away. In these cases, it is possible to set up an alarm system that can warn neighboring cities before the shock waves arrive. The 20 or 30 seconds warning can allow factories, power stations, and other mechanical operations to be safely shut down. Most of the damage from an earthquake comes from the fires proceeding the quake. With this system, potentially dangerous fires can be minimized by shutting down all energy-intensive operations.

Conclusions

	Given the nature of an earthquake occurence, it is
unlikely that we will ever be able to predict earthquakes with
100% accuracy.  However, by aggregating the signs from various
sources such as surface deformation, previous statistics, earthquake
motion data, and precurosry phenomena, we can increase the probability
of a successful prediction.
