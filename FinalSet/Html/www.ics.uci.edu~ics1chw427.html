The Internet? The SuperHighway? Maybe I'll just stay home and watch TV...

Author: Brenda Brooks

E-mail: eahu426@ea.oac.uci.edu
Major: Film Studies

With the Internet leading us into what futurist , Alvin Toffler refers to as "The Third Wave" (or the "Knowledge Age"), in which the new economy and power will be gaged by our capacity to access information, new theories are surfacing both leery of and enthusiastic about our latest evolving medium.

Developed as a means of accessing, navigationally, the tremendous amount of information available on the Internet, the World Wide Web offers an unbelievable amount of resources in the form of "Web sites" or " hypertext documents" without having to know a specialized computer-language. In addition, its means of display has come to incorporate hypermedia devises - such as in-line imaging, audio files, and selectable formats - which has made this access to knowledge even more engaging. With point-and-click instant access to the latest research and information throughout the world, all available through what feels more like an interactive monitor than a complex computer, words such as "empowering" and "democratizing" have begun to surface in terms of its access-ability to the non-computer-literate population. Stories have begun to circulate of people, disenchanted with education or society - even gang members - finding themselves drawn toward the involving nature of this new medium. As opposed to the broadcast model we have been accustomed to for decades, which seems to have bred passivity, consumerism, and the generating of lowest-common-denominator programming in an attempt to reach the widest audiences, a new thirst for interactive forms of "stimulus" (whether via on-line communication or virtual technologies) is emerging - a desire to "investigate" beyond what is initially offered. With people's ability to choose the roles they wish to play, whether as consumers, providers, or both, this new medium holds the promise of activism, democracy, critical thinking, and quality through competition (as long as it remains independent).

Despite these positive effects, there have been many arguments surrounding the Internet and Web regarding representation, social issues, commercialism, regulation/censorship, physical architecture, and power. While these issues have roused theorists in every discipline, for the scope of this paper, only a partially list is represented below. Further, as each group's insights are understandably extensive, I will attempt to relay a fair assessment of their philosophy or analytical perspective.

One group of theorists approach the Net with Marshall McLuhan -based media analysis. McLuhan, best known for his "The Medium is The Message" and "Understanding Media" ,wrote extensively on the nature of media and its social-psychological effects. Meeting regularly in Toronto at the McLuhan Research Centre , the group discusses the Information Hwy, from social issues to highly technical issues. An over-view of their discussions covers areas such as:

  1. a front-end for the information highway
  2. just in time learning
  3. distance education
  4. systematic treatment of space using retrospective
    GIS (Geographical Information Systems)
  5. systematic treatment of time with respect to telepresence in 2-D and 3-D
  6. integration of various tools
The over-view Web site, though not heavily linked, is detailed and fairly substantial.

A related group is comprised of students at the University of Pennsylvania who have organized an experimental course called "Netsurf: the Discussion Seminars". The course Web site, entitled "Social Implications of Information Technologies" includes topics such as:

  • History of Media Technologies
  • Social Psychology of Media Effects
  • Current issues: copyright law and software piracy
  • Current issues: Privacy, Cryptography, and the Panopticon
  • Future Directions.
Within each topic are links to various documents pertaining to those issues. The course approaches each subject with three underlying themes:
  1. The Filtering Function - ask: source? medium? filter? target?"
  2. Synthesis vs Analysis - what is the part? what is the whole? how do they relate? - do we perhaps sacrifice depth for breadth of knowledge?
  3. Do your own research - Browse, search, ask. Be creative.
The course page offers a partially linked fairly extensive reading list, as well, ranging from Marshall McLuhan and Alvin Toffler theoretical books to Wired and Time magazine interviews with other techno. or media-specialists.

The National Academy of Sciences created the National Academy Press to publish reports issued by the National Academy of Sciences, the National Academy of Engineering, the Institute of Medicine, and the National Research Council. "Realizing the Information Future: The Internet and Beyond" is one of NAP's publications discussing the lastest issues concerning the National Information Infrastructure (NII). Areas of discussion include defining the purpose of the NII, social and economic implications, funding arrangements, management responsibilities, increasing commercial service, technical issues, and choosing an appropriate physical infrastructure. Topics overlap some of those on the G7 Live Home Page (Group of Seven Conference Coverage) - another unbelievable site with the latest discussions regarding the "Information Superhwy".

The Progress and Freedom Foundation is a Republican based organization who, while striving to be analytical in its various publications, is nevertheless, strictly pro-progress - "an organization whose aim is to create a positive vision of the future". With its board of directors' and fellows' backgrounds lying in economics, politics, military, and foreign affairs many of their projects carry economic and political overtones such as their Magna Carta for the Knowledge Age" referred to often by House Speaker Newt Gingrich (R-GA). Their approach to the new medium, then, focuses mainly on public policy and information technology.
