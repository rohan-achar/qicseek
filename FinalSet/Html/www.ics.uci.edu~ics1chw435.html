Introduction


The Internet is an electronic world of resources. It offers people a simple way to both access information all over the world and bring that information to their fingertips. FTP allows access to countless files and programs; Telnet enables people to access remote computer sites; Gopher helps to locate information that exists on the Internet; Usenet establishes countless newsgroups in cyberspace that allows users to communicate on a wide range of subjects; and the World Wide Web provides a connected library of files, documents, and resources that can be linked to one another by the touch of a button. While the application of these resources is seemingly endless, there is a distinct interest worldwide in utilizing the Internet's capabilities for the purpose of education.

The Internet's resources provide a bright future for advancements in education. Through the use of the Internet, students are able to have at their fingertips an entire library's worth of resources for research and studying. Instead of learning from just one textbook, students can use the information from hundreds of text materials, each offering a different approach to a similar subject. The opportunites created by the Internet is such that the scope of a child's education is no longer confined to the walls of the classroom, but rather is opened to the unending growth of information and resources in cyberspace. However, the idea of a virtual classroom also creates concerns about abuse of the freedom the Internet provides to the students and the sacrifice of student-teacher interaction that is made with a computerized curriculum.

This paper will examine these complex issues that underlay the basic application of Internet education. On one hand, the sheer capacity of information and resources that students and teachers alike can access through the Net is unsurpassed and should be utilized to its utmost effectiveness. On the other hand, the convention of classroom education offers some essential elements that cannot be achieved or duplicated on a computer screen. This paper aims to examine just how well the Internet can duplicate and improve a conventional classroom curriculum and, further, to highlight the necessary human elements that are unable to be emulated by computers. While the concept of a virtual classroom is slowly becoming a reality, the consequences of such a powerful educational medium must be considered.

Designing the Virtual Classroom

The primary goal in designing a virtual classroom is to make as many resources as possible available to students and teachers. In addition, these resources must be easily accessible and easy to use. With this premise in mind, I have taken the steps to emulate a typical high school curriculum using the services available on the Internet. The curriculum consists of five main subjects: Algebra, English Literature, Biology, U.S. History, and Spanish. The purpose in this simulation is twofold. First, I intend to establish the effectiveness of the Internet's resources in providing a comparable level of education to the level currently available in conventional classrooms. Second, I will show the ways in which virtual learning offers opportunities to teachers and students that are not available in today's centers of education. In order to avoid simply creating a list of relevant sites, I will keep the emulations simple, yet effective. I have included a more comprehensive list of resources at the end of the paper that can also be used to facilitate learning in the virtual classroom.

Algebra

There are two elements involved in teaching and learning mathematics. First, the concepts and the logic of the mathematical science need to be explained in a discernible manner. Second, the student must complete a series of exercises and problems in order to practically understand the concepts. Both of these elements can be successfully achieved on the Internet. The mathematical core of cyberspace is located at Mathematics City where students can access many mathematical resources such as sample problems, games, lesson plans, discussion groups, graphical displays, and, of course, a calculator. Other links can provide students with plenty of other resources to assist them in learning and practicing math.

English Literature

The Internet is a tremendous learning tool for literature. The typical english student spends hours searching in libraries and bookstores for literary works. With the touch of a button, a student in the virtual classroom has instant access to an On-Line Bookstore and an On-Line Library where Hypertext links allow the student to search through catalogs of titles from Charles Dickens to William Shakespeare. The student could also log on to the English Grammar Newsgroup to get help and information on writing, comprehension, and grammar. By discussing books, authors, and literature with people around the world, students are rewarded with varying approaches to and interpretations of literary texts. The ability to access books at the touch of a button also helps to keep students interested in reading and exploring literature.

Biology

Imagine a biology student having access to over sixty different discussion groups, each covering a unique topic in biology. That resource is available through Usenet to students in the virtual classroom. Not only does this allow students to access information on all types of biology, but it also allows them to communicate with students at other schools from over the world about a common interest in the subject. The virtual cu rriculum can even include a step by step computerized Frog Dissection . Again, the capability of having so much information at one's fingertips elevates a student's learning po tential to a different level.

U.S. History

History is a dry, often boring subject because it is simply a recollection of events that happened in the past. Computerized multimedia is a great way to cure the monotony of a history class. In cyberspace, students can find information on any historica l event in the thousands of sites devoted to history. Instead of reading from a textbook, students can embark on an historical adventure through American history by searching through The Scrapbook of American His tory presented by the Library of Congress. Everyday a student could take a field trip to the Interactive Natural History Museum and learn something different about life in the past. The interactive style of c omputerized education places the student in the driver's seat and allows him/her to explore through various historical subjects and events. This freedom is beneficial in a subject such as history, however it is not feasible in a conventional classroom to have this type of access to such a broad range of interesting places.

Spanish

Since learning a foreign language is achieved predominantly through memorization, the computer is a wonderful tool for Spanish students. Students can log on to a Virtual Spanish Classroo m and receive a lesson plan complete with vocabulary, pronunciation, and translation exercises. With the help of multimedia, students can also hear audio files of certain pronunciations so that they get a first hand connection with the language. Hypertext links allow you to jump from lesson to lesson so that you can learn at your own pace. Although there are still limited sites for certain languages, the applications continue to grow.

Perhaps the most useful resource offered on the Internet for foreign language students is other net users from around the world. Because cyberspace is an internationally accessed de stination, Spanish students can talk or send mail to people in South America or Spain over the internet. In addition, students have the opportunity to travel abroad to places around the world. By experienc ing the people, the sites, and the culture of a country, students gain another dimension to learning a language. 


As you can see, it is definitely feasible to establish an effective curriculum using the Internet's resources.  As more and more scholars and educators become familiar with the Internet, th



e resources and services that cater towards education will grow exponentially.  Never before has information been packaged in a way that with a simple click of a button, information is linked together and immediately accessible all over the world.  While 



we can only guess and wonder about the future applications of this phenomenal resource, the current direction of computerized education should be examined closely and carefully.

Pros and Cons of Computerized Education


While the application of computerized education becomes more and more of a reality, it is essential to examine the effects of this advancement on the educational system itself.  After all, with all of the developments made in the educational arena, non



e is more drastic nor more significant than the computerization of the educational curriculum. Because of the grand scale of this change, the cost benefits must be considered before such change is implemented.


The positive aspects of computerizing education is fundamental and obvious.  Today, the major restriction placed on a student's ability to learn or a teacher'
s ability to teach is the resources that are available to both teacher and student.  The resources available to teachers today are limited by shrinking budgets and restricted space. These limitations directly effect the standard of education that students



 receive.  The Internet offers both teachers and students the ability to broaden the pool of resources available to them.  Teachers are able to use 
Usenet  to gain important communication with other educators from around the world.  Also, FTP and the World Wide Web off



er teachers access to a broad range of lesson plans, text materials, and other course related materials that bring new insight and approaches to their curricula. The st



udents are the ones that benefit most from the virtual classroom.  The  Internet opens them up to a world of  information and references.   Students can



 use Usenet  to join worldwide newsgroups which allow them to discuss tons of educational related topics. FTP providess access to educational programs, files, and mailing lists that can  enhance the scope of any subject.  Perhaps the most useful resource ava



ilable to students is the World Wide Web.  Not only does the web present students with computerized libraries, lecture halls, and interactive learning groups, but the Web's unique
HTML hypertext format allows students to continually find new links to more educational resources on t



he  web.  The perpetual increase in resources and services that the hypertext links provide for both students and teachers is the single most significant e



lement of the virtual classroom because it connects students and educators with the latest information and resources available.


There are, of course, some elements of a conventional classroom that cannot be emulated by a computer. For example, physical interaction with teachers and classmates cannot be achieved through a computer.  While interaction and communication between tw



o people is possible on the Internet, the physical exchange is lost. Although this may appear to be a simple sacrifice, the ability to physically interact with others is an integral part of



 a child's maturity and therefore is an element that should be included in the child's education.  Further,  there are situations in the classroom where hands on experience is necessary in order for a student to understand the work.  Subjects such as art,



 ceramics, physical education, chemistry, and auto shop would be impossible to learn solely on a computer because hands on experience is necessary.  Students could use the Internet to gain 



information about these subjects, but they would not be able to use the keyboard to do the exercises, make the pottery, fix the engine, paint the canvas, or mix the chemicals.  Although some strides have been made to emulate hands on activities on a compu



ter screen (such as computerized
 frog dissections), there is still an inevitable void in terms of hands on experience in avirtual classroom.  


The fundamental difference between conventional classroom education and a computerized curriculum is one of single versus multiple dimensions of application  Classrooms are one dimensional in the sense that students and teachers only have access to the



 tools and resources that exist in the confines of the classroom walls.  Computers, on the other hand, offer limitless boundaries to the scope of information and services available to students and educators.This after all is the purpose and intent of Cybe



rspace.  However, when it comes to physical interaction and hands on experience, the classroom offers more dimensions than the computer offers.  Ideally, the two exclusive elements will be combined in a way that the strengths of each entity will be emphas



ized and a completely multi-dimensional classroom environment will be achieved.

Conclusion




The primary element of education is the distribution of information.  In today's society, people go to school in order to get an education because that is where information is distributed.  However, the technological advancements in computers and speci



fically in the Internet threaten to change the protocol of this distribution.  Simply put, information can be distributed more effectively, more efficiently, and more abundantly on a comput



er than in a classroom.  Ironically, the degree to which the Net becomes an institution of learning is directly contingent upon the number of people who become educated about the Internet itself.  If people do not make themselves familiar with the Internet and its capabilities, then there will be nobody to take advantage of the resources available.  The technological revolution that has flourished in the past twenty years has made speed and



 efficiency the primary elements of success in our society.  Consequently, the speed and efficiency that the virtual classroom brings to education will be the catalyst for academic success in the future.

Unfortunately, the human element will never be fully emulated on a computer screen.  Even with the recent developments in multimedia, where live images can be transmitted from computer to computer, a screen cannot replace the effect or the impact of pe



rsonal interaction. The reality is that the physical interaction of student and teacher is a necessary aspect of learning that cannot be sacrificed.  As it was stated earlier, the greatest improvements in the education system will come as a result of the 



integration of human and computer resources. While the technology is available today, the application and implementation of this technology will have to wait until we decide that we cannot do without it.
