What Is A Comet?

Comets are commonly known as "dirty snowballs" or large balls of cosmic dust and ice. They are left over pieces of astral matter that for one reason or another was not involved in the formation of planets or moons when the universe was first born. A comet is formed around a centrally located disk called a coma. Surrounding the coma are many fragments of elments that are commonly found in space. The "tail" of the comet is consistes of hydrogen, carbon, nitrogen, and oxygen. All of these radicals can be broken away from methane, ammonia, and water which all can exist as ices. What is also intetresting to note is that comets do not follow Newtonian gravitational laws which suggests the theory that the escaping gases produce a force which hurls the comet through space. Short lived comets also lose force with time, which suggests that they are similar to burning torches, running on only a given amount of fuel. The head of a comet, including the hazy coma, can exceed the size of earth. That is a scary thought, considering that one day a comet may colliode with our planet.

How The Sun Effects Comets

A comet is approaching the sun by way ofa great gravitational pull. As the ball of gases and ice gets nearer to the sun's extreme heat, the high temperatures cause the comet to become visible. The molecules of the comet are blown away by the solar wind at a speed of 250 miles per second. As a comet's orbit takes it away from the sun, the reduced heat and solar wind causes the tail to receed. So all of the comets that yuou and I see pictures of are neer the sun, since the tails are long and magnificent. The nearer to the sun, the more visible the comet. The sun's gravitational pull causes comets to have an elliptical orbit. The orbits are so vast that it carries the astral body far out of the solar system. There is also a direct relationship between comets and meteor showers. When a comet comes relatively close to earth, the debris given off by the tail creates a meteor shower as it falls into our atmosphere.
