Introduction

There is a fierce fight going on in the Russian Caucasian territory of Chechnya. The Chechens are one of the most unpopular ethnic groups in Russian History. The mainly Moslem territory is under siege by Russian forces attempting to win back the small territory from the rebels. Many different factors seem to be to blame for the uprising as well as the fierce attempt to squelch the insurgents. The United Nations is not getting involved other than to help refugees displaced by the heavy fighting find a decent supply of food and shelter, and to take the burden off of the good natured souls who are now supporting them. New developements are constantly arising in either the political forum in Moscow, on the battlefield in the war torn territory, or on the streets of Russia and the ever-changing support of the population. Many people are unsure on whether they should support the fighting or oppose Yeltsin. It is a tough political stance to take because the motives for the fighting are all questionable. It is well known that the political and economic situation in Russia is very poor and is actually looked upon as a joke by many workers. Yeltsin has made a definite stance on the issue to win back the territory, but the reasons why are not so clear. Another thing that is unsure is the outcome and the eventual costs of the fighting that are sure to plague either country for many years.

The Role of the United Nations

The United Nations does not seem very concerned with the events that are transpiring in Chechnya as of now. The concern with Bosnia and the Serbs is a much greater concern at this point, but it seems that with the apparent slaughter that seems to be the goal of Russia, there would be some cause for intervention or concern on the part of the United Nations. However, it appears that the U.N. is attempting to help the displaced Chechens who fled the country during the fierce fighting and assault on their capital Grozny. The Daily Highlights of the United Nations available on Gopher, mention Chechnya very little, and it apparent that they are not attempting to curb the fighing. they are, however, making a very concentrated effort to help the refugees of the fighting, but as one diplomat says, "There are tens of thousands of people who need help urgently and Moscow recognizes this. Yet it can't get its act together and issue visas." The general consensus is that the U.N. has no part in the political matters of Russia, but the estimated 100,000 refugees are of great concern to them.

Various Views of the War

The atmosphere is constantly changing on the battlefield, as well as in the Kremlin and the streets as far as the consensus of the people. Part of the general attitude of the people surfaces in A Revival For Russian Dissident Chechen War Provides New Reason to Protest, an overview of one woman's ideas which are most likely shared with much of the population. Susanna Pechuro feels that the war with Chechnya is a new genocide. She was alive and protesting against Stalin, which got her thrown in a labor camp, when he deported the Chechens. Now she says that Yeltsin is destroying them. She is not alone in her analysis. Much of the outside media cites evidence of major human rights breaches. According to Voice of America, Yelena Bonner, a human rights activist, has resigned from Russia's Human Rights Commission because President Yeltsin and those around him have abandoned the human rights issue. The death toll, according to VOA's Russian correspondent Peter Collins has reached about 25,000 civilians in almost three months of fighting. That incredible number leads many people to believe that it is a conscious effort to completely destroy the Chechen people.

Another view is told by Professor M. Berlin in Moscow. From talking to English speaking Muscovites, he has received a wide range of ideas concerning the Russian involvement in Chechnya. His initial findings were that there is a feeling that there is not much in Chechnya worth the loss of Russian lives, and that the incompetence displayed is all the fault of Boris Yeltsin. Berlin says " If Yeltsin counted on the Russian bias against Chechens (and others from the Caucasus) to win public support, he miscalculated." Chechnya is a republic and many people just don't see any real reason to be fighting a country in which the people "just do crime" according to one woman. There is much bigotry expressed against the people of Chechnya. This particular woman also seemed to think that "nothing is grown or manufactured in Chechnya, and that the local oil industry cost Russia more than it produces in product." (Berlin) Actually many feel that Chechnya should be given total independence, and expelled from the Russian federation. (Berlin)

Yeltsin, of course, has his own reasons for the invasion of Chechnya. As Margaret Shapiro Reports in her Washington Post Article, Chechnya is a Republic "which unilaterally declared its independence in 1991 and which Russia says has become a base for arms dealers and gangsters." Foreign Minister Andrei Kozyrev justified the military assault on Chechnya by saying that it was prompted by the activity of armed groups, which are totally intolerable in any country." (Shapiro) The purpose, according to Yeltsin, seems to be to reestablish order and the local economy and not to totally destroy the Chechen people.

Political Motives Behind the Fighting

As stated before, it is highly possible that Yeltsin was trying to feed off the Russian people's dislike for the Chechen people when he decided to invade their small Republic, but if that was the case, then it backfired badly. Because of the tough economic situation in Russia as well as the surrounding areas, it is highly possible, as reported by Kunio Sakuma, a Soviet Union Researcher, that "The tough stance taken by the Yeltsin administration in regard to Chechnya should keep 'regional uprisings' under tentative control, but it is quite possible that regional authorities will start to push gradually for an expansion of their own powers." And it is also very possible that Yeltsin feels the economies of Russia and the surrounding areas would not be able to handle a full independence of Russia at this point. Every economy of the area is in bad shape and many people are on strike because there is no money to pay anyone. If all of their resources continue to fall apart, such as the oil supply in Chechnya, then their economic base will be completely destroyed and they will inevitably slip back into the trap of communism.

On the other side of the spectrum, Chechnya has to fight for their own independence in order to finally beat what has plagued them for centuries, being under the rule of an oppressive Russia. Chechens have a sort of genetic distrust of Russia due to the Stalin era as well as ages before of exile and deportation. The desire to be completely independent of an imperialistic nation such as Russia must be absolutely overwhelming for them.

Current Developments

My main sources of updated information are the brief but apparently accurate reports of Voice of America correspondents in Russia. The I have this article in my mail archives because it would no longer be available on-line. The main complaints now are that Russian artillery is hitting the Chechen villages, supposedly killing numerous civilians. This is the reason that many people are making a human rights issue out of the war. Because of the need for Russian troops to expose the Chechens in their home territory, they must fire upon the villages that they are held up in, and if the Chechen citizens have not yet been able to flee, then there are bound to be casualties.

Possible Outcomes

One of the main statements that Yeltsin has been sure to make is that he vows not to punish the Chechens. According to Margaret Shapiro's report, Yeltsin also claimed that the refugees will be allowed to return to their homes. Of course, this is all assuming that Russia wins. This seems inevitable. When they do win, it appears that the only way they are going to be able to maintain any sort of control over Chechnya is to put them under a sort of military rule and control all aspects of their government for a while. But the desire for everyone is to avoid what has happened to the Chechen people over their long history. The fact is that the Chechen people have been punished for their Muslim based religion, their geographical location (being in the way of Russia's path to India and Syria), and their natural resources. When Hitler was enacting his "final solution" upon the Jews, the same thing was happening to the Chechen people. Yeltsin can not let that happen.

If Chechnya miraculously pulls out a victory, they will have quite a struggle with their economy being totally separate from Russia, as well as the obvious trouble that Russia will give them in any economic move that they may make. They respect as an independent nation will be non-existent as will be an effective form of government. Right now, it just seems like a no-win war.
