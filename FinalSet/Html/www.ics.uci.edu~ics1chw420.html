1. Introduction

The World-Wide Web has yet to be fully embraced by educators at the high school and grade school levels. The problem arising from the fact that only a small portion of public high schools and elementary schools have access to the Internet. Once more educators realize the potential of the Internet and the World-Wide Web will there be an increased demand for the web's resources in classrooms. When this happens those currently accessing the web for educational purposes can expect increased research and developement in web technology that will cater more specifically to their needs. Moreover, the World-Wide Web's recent explosion in growth has already made news headlines. This attention the web has gained will hopefully continue to climb. If so, the interest of providing web service to grade school and high school stude nts will likely increase as well.

The driving force behind the use of the World-Wide Web as an instructional tool lies in the web's ability to provide a limitless amount of information through a multimedia interface. A web page further reinforces its own value as a multimedia document by employing its own links to other documents via hypertext.

In the document, Advanced Educational Uses of the World-Wide Web, Stephen Franklin and Bertrand Ibrahim discuss two methods the classroom teacher can employ while using the web as a teaching tool. The first method discussed is to use web technology and corresponding browser technology to create web documents that model normal subject oriented course material or implementing a "closed corpus" strategy. An example of this strategy includes web documents that implement "fill-out forms". These "fill-out forms" allow students to enter data into a web document at specified places, therefore eliminating the need for homework assignments to be placed on paper. Students would submit their answers via the "fill-out form". The second way the web can be used is for specific information retrieval and reference or using an "open corpus" strategy. An example of this strategy would be assigning students to pick a certain subject area to explore on the web, having them browse the web's domain in that area, and submit their own web document about the subject area they were exploring.

The examples given above provide excellent use of web technology and its ability to incorporate distance learning. Distance learning does not take place in a classroom or at a library. Distance learning maintains the idea that one document can serve millions of people who never have to leave a computer terminal to gain that documents information.

A final important note to make is that web documents are online, meaning they can be continuously be updated, changed, and refined, unlike a textbook or any other static physical object. With these points in mind the web's value, as a dynamic and ever expanding resource, cannot be ignored by the remaining majority of our educators.

3. Advantages and Disadvantages

 There are many advantages as well as disadvantages for everything, even in using the Web. An important advantage in using the Web as a tool for teaching is that:

The World Wide Web is a useful browser. It makes it possible for the user to navigate the Net. It also provides links from one information source to another information source. This is very useful to both students and teachers because this gives them a chance to explore exciting educational sites that are available. This also gives them an opportunity to share their knowledge and findings on various interest subjects with people around the world. Thus, it makes learning fun. For example, if students in California wanted to know about the weather or what kinds of damages were created during the earthquake Japan had earlier this year, they can find out by using the programs on the Web. This will give students valuable learning that is fun.

There are also disadvantages of using the Web. For instance, it could be quite confusing because sometimes we are unable to proceed with our navigation within the Web. This is due to the problems with navigational links where there are missing links, errors within the link (because it moved to a different server), or even because of design problems. Another problem would be that there is so much information available out there that you just do not know which ones contain valuable information. This is known as Useless WWW Pages. Similar to worthless web pages are pages that are literally listed as Worst of the Web (TM).

"In an educational context World Wide Web clients such as Mosaic are of limited value because they put learners into the role of information absorbers...Despite the richness of exhibits in terms of the amount of material presented and the use of multimedia, activity is resricted to navigation through a real (museum) or hyper (Mosaic) spaces."

Although there are many problems in using the World Wide Web, it is still considered as a useful tool in education because with the ever increasing demand in science and technology there will be changes and modifications to these programs. Pretty soon there will be newer programs and ways of obtaining educational resources.

4. Security Issues

 There are two main security issues that face educators and developers when using the web in education. The first concern implies that young students who have access to the Internet may obtain pornographic and obscene material. The second security issue concerns web bandwidth. Children, whether they realize it or not, may retrieve enormous amounts of data thousands of miles away without a second thought of what they are doing.

Retrieving documents that contain explicit pictures or words in them is relatively easy and only takes a fair amount of browsing to accomplish. Students who do gain the ability to browse the web can painlessly discover links that can take them to pages similar to The Complete Internet Sex Resource Guide. (A web document that provides access to several adult oriented Internet sites.) The resourcefulness of one student can quickly lead to many students gaining the URL to such a document. Security measures must be initiated to insure that students do not gain access to such things or the internet's community relations could suffer.

There are two obvious security methods to use in guarding students from adult-oriented material on the web. One method is to continually monitor students as they traverse the web. The second is the creation of web technology that can be better protected against unwanted retrievals.

There is one tool already available in the Mosaic browser called kiosk mode. Kiosk mode removes the menubar and all control bar options except back, forward, home and close. Which limits some of the functionality of a particular document but the hypertext links will still be available. So use of the kiosk mode is limited at best.

At the December 1994 Internet Engineering Task Force meeting The IETF HTTP Security Working Group was put into action to develop requirements and specifications for security services for the web's protocol, HTTP. Working groups for WWW/HTTP security are just getting into action as indicated by the Rutgers WWW-Security Index home page.

5. Technical Difficulties

 The main technical difficulties that plague the k-12 educational community are:
  • The ability of the web to deliver high-quality education at great distances over a shorter amount of time. (Children will quickly become irritated during a slow connection.)

  • The development of programs that will allow teachers to effieciently produce quality material for their students use on the World-Wide Web.

Since client programs request services from servers the first technological difficulty lies within web servers. The most crucial part of the server-client relationship is latency. Latency has several factors, including the client program's ability of displaying retrieved data and a server's hardware slowness, however the major problem is the web network's own latency. Latency due to propagation delays. These delays stem from the fact that the Hypertext Transport Protocol (HTTP) makes too many unnecessary round trips when gathering information for a client. Improvements in latency overhead will undoubtably improve the speed at which documets on the web can be accessed and therefore displayed. A notable effort in Improving HTTP Latency was turned in by Venkata N. Padmanabhan (University of California-Berkeley) and Jeffrey C. Mogul (Digital Equipment Corporation Western Research Laboratory), their idea to improve latency by making simple changes to the HTTP protocol and its implementations resulted in significantly improved response times.

The second technological difficulty is provding programs to help teachers develop educational material for their students. An excellent example of a walkthru guide on how a teacher could set up the World-Wide Web for his/her classroom is the Web66 Internet Server Kitchen. web pages such as this one and improvements in the technology of "fill-out forms" as mentioned in Advanced Educational Uses of the World_Wide Web are just the beginnig of the offerings that the web will soon be producing for primary and secondary educators.
