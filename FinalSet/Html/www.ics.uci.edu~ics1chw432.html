One major topic of controversy with the topic of personal freedoms on the internet is that of the Clipper chip. "Clipper", you say, "wasn't that a TV show about a dolphin." No, that's Flipper. The Clipper chip is an algorithm for encrypting data that the government wants all user of the internet to utilize. What is data encryption? Well, that's a good place to start.

Data encryption is any means by which someone disguises or otherwise makes unreadable any piece of data. The various ways of encrypting data are referred to as algorithms. For instance, one alogrithm for encrypting a message would be to shift the letters of the message by a certain number, say three. Hence the word "encode" would be represented by "hqfrgh." 'H' represents 'e', 'q' represents 'n', 'f' represents 'c', and so on--each letter shifted three forward letters. The algorithm would then be said to be shifting by n letters, and in this case, n is 3. The next question is how the receiver of the message unencrypt it. That person is given what is referred to as the "key." In this case the key would be said to be 3. Who should have access to the key? That is major source of controversy surrounding the Clipper chip.

The Clipper chip is the algorithm developed by the government to encrypt voice transmissions. Its counterpart in data encryption is Capstone, the device that influence much of the internet. Though people usually refer to both of them as the Clipper chip. The National Security Agency supports the Clipper chip quite simply so the government will have access to it. If everyone uses different data encryption algorithms, the government will not be able to regulate the internet as much as it would like to. For instance, if a criminal is sending information over the internet, the goverment will not be able understand what he sending. However with the Clipper chip, the government would have access to encrypted files. Without it they may still use wiretaps, but it won't do them any good, because the data is encrypted. How would the government be able to read the files without allowing anyone to do the same? It works kind of like those locks used in schools. Each student knows the combination to only his lock, but the teacher has a master key that allows access to all of the locks. So each person using the Clipper chip will be able to unencrypt only his own messages, while the government will have a "master code" that will unencrypt any transmission encrypted with the Clipper chip.

Most users of data encryption are entirely opposed to the thought of the government having a means to read any file they choose. This opposition comes from many sources--from average people to prestigious companies:

  • The U.S. Association for Computing Machinery's opposition to the Clipper chip is twofold:
    1. As an encryption device it will hinder technological development and place U.S. companies behind foreign companies that will likely not have to adhere to national standards.
    2. Communications security is too important to be left to secret processes and classified algorithms. They must be developed openly and with full public participation.
  • The Computer Professionals for Social Responsibility oppose the Clipper chip on the grounds that:
    1. The government will create extensive obstacles for companies with intentions to incresase technology in the area of data encryption.
    2. In a formal request for comments from the Department of Commerce, very few replied in favor of it. Several hundred opposed it.
  • On the whole, most people oppose on the following grounds:
    1. It is an infringement on personal freedom to be required to use any given encryption method. A person may feel he has an algorithm that is more effective. If the Clipper chip becomes mandatory, he will be denied this right.
    2. It may not be a good enough algorithm. If someone cracked it or otherwise got their hands on the key, they would be able to read any file encrypted with the Clipper chip, which would be all files. That is tremendous power.
    3. People quite simply don't want the government to have the capability to read any file any American sends. It's a simple matter of privacy.
