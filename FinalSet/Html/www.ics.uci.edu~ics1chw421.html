Introduction


There are many different kinds of thievery in our society; most noticeable are the stealing of merchandise, money, or anything else of value. However, with the rise of high-tech computers in the 80's and 90's, a new kind of crime emerged along with it. The act of stealing computer software, known as piracy, has been growing out of proportion in the past few years. As a result of this, software companies are losing billions of dollars in lost revenue every year for which the legitimate consumer ultimately suffers with higher prices. Many attempts were made by both the government and software companies to counter this trend, but to no avail. Time is running out and as our world economy becomes increasingly dependent on information and technology, protecting intellectual property is essential for our future. In this paper, I will explore the world of computer piracy along with possible solutions for resolving it.


Definition


Computer piracy, by definition, is the unauthorized duplication of computer software. It can occur when a person purchases software and duplicates it in order to give it to their friends, relatives, or whomever. That person can then make more copies and give it to their friends which perpetuates this ongoing cycle. Another way in which unauthorized distribution takes place is through illegal computer bulletin board services (BBS) that distribute hundreds of copyrighted commercial software as well as pornography and other censored material. Computer piracy does not only occur between private friends and BBS's, but also through corporate sites where an enormous number of pirated software is distributed.

How has it come this far?


The reason that computer piracy has become so big is because it is so easy to get away with. First of all, it only takes a computer and an inexpensive blank disk in order to duplicate the particular software for distribution. Second, since the information on diskettes are recorded digitally, the precision and integrity of the software's code is always perfect. Digital information can be copied at no cost and with practically no detection since the 1000th copy made of a particular software will be indistinguishable from the original.


Facts and Figures


It is estimated that the software industry lost more than $12.8 billion due to piracy with $2.2 billion in the U.S. alone according to the Business Software Alliance. They also estimate that 35% of the total software in the U.S. may be illegal, 80% in Japan, and an incredible 99% in Thailand. With staggering figures like these, the software companies have no other choice but to raise the prices on their software in order to make up for the lost revenue.


Legal Issues


Stopping computer piracy or even controlling piracy alone is a very difficult task as it was evident in the past events. At first, the government proposed and passed different kinds of laws discouraging computer piracy. According to the U.S. Copyright Law, computer piracy is a federal offense and civil damages can be as much as $50,000 or more and criminal penalties include fines and imprisonment. This may seem like a pretty tough law, but unfortunately people ignore it because computer piracy is very easy to get away with. Consequently, the government got specific by passing a legislation that made possessing ten unauthorized copies of a software program a felony.

Past Failed Attempts


With new laws proving to be ineffective, software developers tried to counter computer piracy by developing a technique called 'copy-protection'. They tried to outsmart software thieves by either altering tracks on the diskette in order to make it harder to duplicate or making the software perform 'document checks' in which the program requests particular information from the owner's manual of the software in order to run the program. This would insure that the person currently running the program is the legitimate owner. This also eventually became a futile attempt by software companies; highly skilled computer hackers used their knowledge to remove the copy- protection. After all of this, it is evident that new strict laws by the government nor schemes to outsmart potential thieves by the software industry do not intimidate the hackers any bit.


Roots to the problem


When trying to resolve computer piracy, you must first look at the roots that contribute to it. In analyzing the causes of computer piracy that I have already mentioned, it can be broken down to two main factors. First, one source of computer piracy is between private citizens (between friends and BBS's). Second, another main source of computer piracy occurs on corporate sites.


Ethics and Moral Issues


Computer piracy is very common among private citizens. people can make copies for their friends, family, or just about anyone. Unfortunately, many people innocently feel that they are doing nothing wrong by making a few copies for their friends. They do not realize that they are not only doing a disservice to themselves by breaking the law, but also by causing everyone to suffer from the increase in prices on future software. When a consumer legally purchases a piece of software from a vendor, there is a contract that exists between the software company and the consumer. Most people believe that once they have purchased software, they own it. This is not quite true. In fact, what has been purchased is the license allows the purchaser to use the product. The software company still owns the software. This lack of understanding often leads to breaches of contract through software piracy, and in many cases the pirates are not even aware of the illegalities of their actions. We must also understand that there is a mutual trust between the consumer and the software developer; "the consumer trusts that the software developer will develop the best possible software along with the fairest prices and the software developer trusts that the consumer will use the software only in the manner for which they have purchased it" and will not make any unauthorized copies.(SPA)

Possible Solutions?


We must educate the consumers, here and abroad, on the legal and ethical issues of computer piracy. Although a lot of attention is given to the new computer technology, computer piracy is rarely addressed in the media or in the educational institutions. Raising the public's awareness about computer piracy is a very realistic proposal and can be executed without much hassle. One prime example who are already executing this plan are the two major anti- piracy organizations: Business Software Alliance (BSA) and Software Publishers Association (SPA). One objective for both the BSA and the SPA is education; they want the public to understand what computer piracy is. In order to do this, these organizations have printed numerous brochures about piracy and also made public messages on television to inform the public about what is going on. The SPA have also set up a 1-800# for the public in which they can call with questions dealing with piracy and its legalities. I believe that the government can also take part in this by making publicized messages on television/radio and even implement them through education in the schools. These are both good means of getting the message out to the public.

In dealing with big corporations about computer piracy, I think that actions and litigation must be put forth against them. Non-profit organization such as the SPA are the leading groups that help litigate and convict corrupt corporations. These SPA alone filed 245 lawsuits for illegal software copying, had 332 cease and desist orders issued, had 577 organizations targeted for investigation, and fined $3.6 million in total fines to corporate sites in 1993 alone (Brandel 12). With these numbers, we can see that the SPA has done a substantial job into fighting piracy for a single organization.

And the Rest of the World?


Even if the United States succeeds in the fight against piracy, the rest of the world still remains a big problem. Computer piracy is even worse in Asia, Europe, and other parts of the world. In some countries, laws do not exist in which the unauthorized duplication of copyrighted material is prohibited. Even though 80% of all the software in the world is from the United States, we cannot simply act as the "world police" and enforce its own laws on other countries. I believe that the U.S. government should get involved and try to communicate with the governments of other countries in order to help prevent piracy abroad. We should encourage foreign countries to enforce its copyright laws if they do have one and establish copyright laws if they don't have one.

Conclusion


In conclusion, when we mention the word "pirate", two different versions of it come to our minds. The first are the ruthless and violent men on a ship who stole treasures from other people hundreds of years ago. The second thing that comes to mind is a person that illegally duplicates computer software without buying it. It may be difficult to draw a relationship from these two viewpoints, but the act of either piracy is pretty much the same thing. It is theft. Both were and are illegal and both hurts the legitimate owner. In dealing with the piracy that occurs nowadays, many attempts will be made in the future in trying to prevent software piracy. No one single solution will ever completely stop computer piracy instantly. However, if we look at the different roots of piracy and try to tackle each problem one at a time, I believe we can do the next best thing for our future.
