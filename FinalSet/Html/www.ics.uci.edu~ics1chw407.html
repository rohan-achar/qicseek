LAW SCHOOLS ON THE WEB

The World Wide Web has provided the opportunity for thousands and thousands of users to access knowledge about virtually any topic. The popularity of the web is due in large part to the range of knowledge that is accessible. The depth of knowledge in individual areas has been surprisingly good. Part of the reason for this is because individual companies, institutions and agencies contribute to various fields.

The field that I am most interested involves the legal field, and more specifically, law schools. I have used the web over the course of the last several weeks to access information about law schools and more importantly, what they can provide to web users. Of most importance, I tried to answer the following questions:

  1. What law schools provide information to web users?
  2. Does the presentation or quality of this information differ from what is available through non-electronic means?
  3. Which schools put out information of higher quality?
  4. Which schools are pioneers in the field in terms of new and interesting ways to provide information via electronic means?

What I found was a bit surprising. I entered this project with the assumption, however misinformed, that the better the law school, the better the web page. And my assumptions also led me to believe that if a law school was not highly regarded in legal circles, it would in all probability have a web page that was rather non-spectacular. Well my assumptions could not be further from the truth. The top two law schools in the country, Yale and Harvard, do not have web pages but rely on a gopher menu instead. These menus are poorly developed and are not nearly as useful as some of the services provided by other law schools. Conversely, one of the most interesting and innovative web pages is from Case Western Reserve University law school, ranked nowhere near the elite U.S. law schools.

My categorization of U.S. law schools that have web pages can be separated into three distinct groups. The first group is for law schools that are just getting started with the web. These law schools lack the innovation of others, but are to be commended for experimenting with the web. The second group is comprised of law schools that offer intesting and detailed web pages that are useful for web users and prospective students. These pages provide text and sometimes pictures that are helpful in learning about the school. The third group is comprised of the elite U.S. law schools in terms of their web pages, but not necessarily their legal reputation. These schools have gone far beyond the typical web page and have experimented with innovative pages that are very useful to web users. They are the legal pioneers of the web community. My findings about law school web pages can be summarized by answering my four initial questions posed at the beginning of this paper.

Questions I Have Asked

  1. What law schools provide information to web users?

    Unfortunately, less than half of the U.S. law schools provide pages for interested web users. Courtesy of the University of Southern California Law Library, a listing of law schools on the web has been made available for those interested. Although the University of Southern California listing is the most comprehensive, there are other services which provide much of the same information. Stanford University Law School provides another listing of law schools on the web that is virtually identical to USC's listing. John Marshall Law School also provides a detailed representation of law schools available on the web. The John Marshall listing is unique because it differentiates the law schools based on whether or not they are a world wide web server or a gopher server. Another listing of law schools on the web is provided by Rutgers University Law School which lists the schools as part of its extensive web page. Finally, there is a listing of law schools and law firms on the world wide web that is provided by Indiana University law school. This listing also contains law schools overseas in such places as Great Britain and Germany.

  2. Does the presentation or quality of this information differ from what is available through non-electronic means?

    For some law school web pages, the information available is no more particularly valuable than what is offered in catalog form (in some cases, the information is less worthwhile than printed information). One example of a modest web page is the one offered by Whittier Law School. This page offers a brief description of the law school but there is little substantive information about courses, faculty or admissions statistics. There are no pictures of the law school or surrounding area.

    Perhaps the most pathetic menu is the one offered by one of the elite U.S. law schools: Harvard Law School. This web page has no graphics or pictures, not to mention a comprehensive table of contents. The only thing that is offered is a listing of courses. The course information is presented in simple text form and could use some additional work. The only law school in the country that is ranked higher than Harvard is Yale.

    Yale's gopher-limited menu is rather mundane. The information is standard and the presentation is non-spectacular. The gopher menu that was produced by Yale is for the entire college rather than the law school itself. Yale was kind enough to include a Yale search directory so I typed in 'law school' but was not overwhelmed with the results of my search. The difference between web pages and gopher services really begins to show.

  3. Which schools put out information of higher quality?

    Many law schools are beginning to put out information that is obviously of higher quality. These web pages include pertinent information about the faculty, school, admissions and the surrounding community. One example of this is the web page put forth by Stanford University Law School. Using this web page, users can access information about the study of law at Stanford, student organizations, the faculty and deans and financial aid. More personal information such as child care and health services is also available.

    Another California law school, Hastings College of the Law, has distinguished itself by including a photograph of the law school on its web page (The University of Cincinnati College of Law also provides a photograph of the law school). Another useful tool on the Hastings web page is the feedback form for comments. For current legal topics, web users can access the Hastings Women's Law Journal. This service provides "critical perspectives" on current topics such as the O.J. Simpson trial. One of the law schools with a comprehensive description of its library is the Tarlton Law Library from the University of Texas at Austin.

  4. Which schools are pioneers in the field in terms of new and interesting ways to provide information via electronic means?

    There are three law schools that I believe to be the legal pioneers on the web. The first is the University of Washington School of Law. I have selected this law school because of its listing of internet law resources. This service provides links for web users to Supreme Court decisions, U.S. Government Hypertexts and the American Civil Liberties Union. All of these services are useful and they help to enhance the quality of the University of Washington home page.

    The second law school home page that is noticeably better than most others is the one provided by the University of Southern California Law Center and Law Library. This web page provides access to federal legal resources as well as California legal resources. USC did not stop there however. There are also International Legal Materials and United Nations Resources available. These research tools are very useful for people interested in legal issues. Unfortunately, the legal resources offered by U.S.C. do not include a comprehensive listing of judicial decisions. This can only be obtained by services such as Westlaw and Lexus that charge for log-on time.

    The last pioneer law school on the web is Case Western Reserve Law School. Case Western is a pioneer for a couple of reasons. First of all, it provides a comprehensive list of Internet Law Related Libraries. This may not instantly provide information to users, but it provides hypertext links to libraries that can. Another innovation from Case Western is the ability to request application materials from the law school on-line. Perhaps the next step will be to actually apply to law schools by using the web. Finally, for those interested in international law, Case Western provides a Digest of the International Law of the United States. All three of these services are interesting and useful to the law student or prospective student.

Currently, there are only a few law schools that provide extensive access to legal knowledge via the world wide web. However, these schools are successful in providing a wide range of knowledge and have already established a foundation for other law schools to follow. Eventually, law schools should be able to provide a wide range of services for web users. For instance, prospective students could apply to law schools on-line. Comprehensive judicial decisions could be made available for web users free of charge. Each law school could also include law review articles as part of their web page in order to assist web users with research. All of these services will help to expand the usefulness of these law school web pages and at the same time increase the importance of the world wide web. Eventually, as the services of the world wide web expand, the need for non-electronic information will become greatly diminished. Already, we are seeing the tremendous demand for information via the world wide web.
