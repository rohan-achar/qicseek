The internet has become a very important tool for society. Its importance is of both academic and commercial interests. This research project will focus on the emergence of commercial uses for the web. Specific areas that will be addressed will include: what is available now, how accessible are businesses, how electronic transactions will be conducted, and finally how on-line business advertise that is different than that of regular businesses.

To begin with, there are many limiting factors to doing business on the internet. These factors limit how much can actually be conveyed over the electronic lines. For instance, there is the issue of accessibility. Not everyone has a computer or even regular access to one. This severely limits the size of your potential customer base. On the other hand this market is rapidly growing.

Secondly, if you do have access to a personal computer or access to a direct internet connection, the capabilities of browsers are limiting factors as well. For instance, navigating the world wide web can be demanding because of the direct link required to run powerful browsers like Mosaic. Mosaic provides an excellent interface, however, access to this program is severely limited. Even beyond the accessibility problem, the availability of add-on's to browsers are another factor. Add-on's entitle the browser to sound or graphic display capabilities. Any of these add- ons can change the way in which information is conveyed over the computer terminal.

Lastly, searching the World Wide Web can be cumbersome. Until recently and the introduction of search engines such as Harvest and Lycos, gathering information was a very tedious task. These engines will help users find information that is valuable and allow people to "surf" the web much faster and more efficiently.

Electronic cash

With the emergence of making transactions over the network, one form of "electronic cash" has been created to help facilitate anonymous payments, "just as if both parties shared the same room." Abbreviated as ecash, this system was developed by DigiCash Inc., and is a software solution for payment rather than the traditional use of money. Making transactions using ecash can be made easily, privately and safely due to advances in cryptography.

The use of this electronic payment system is considered more secure than giving your credit card to a waiter in a restaurant, because "electronic cash is digitally 'signed' by the issuer" and processed immediately. Privacy is an inherent part of the system because unlike paper cash, electronic cash is virtually untraceable. However, users can easily prove if they did or did not make a payment, making fraudulent activities almost non-existent. Payment can be made from any computer connected to the internet when coupled with the use of a special client program.

Ecash is a form of currency that is currently being beta tested and is very possible of being widely used by many vendors on the net because of its advantages. In order for the ecash system to work, 4 parties are needed: 1) an issuing bank that will exchange real money for ecash; 2) users who will spend the ecash; 3) vendors who will accept ecash as payment for goods and services; and 4) banks that will clear payments received from vendors.

"Virtual retail stores" such as DigiCash Cybershops are already accepting ecash as payment. For example, there's a games shop where you can play simple games like Tic Tac Toe online, and if you win, you get ecash back. The Samaritans of Cheltenham and District is a charitable organization in the U.K. that accepts real money or ecash as donations.

With already the existence of "virtual retail stores" like the ones located inside Virtual Mall,, product ordering has not been well established yet, but orders can be printed to a printerat the vendor's site, emailing, or appending the order to a text file. For example, when ordering a pizza from Pizza Hut, you provide your name, address, and phone number, and if your area (determined from your area code) is being serviced, then you will be presented with a menu with pull-down fields. The order will then be emailed to a server, which will then be forwarded to the local location.

An experimental grocery store called Wane's Online Grocery Store allows you to grab a shopping cart and go into different "aisles" where you can choose items you want to purchase. It keeps a running total of your purchases. When you're ready to checkout, just hit the "cash register" button, and you can pay for your groceries using ecash.

Advertising on the net

With a growing number of net-users, businesses have made the move toward targeting the massive market of web enthusiasts. In todays age, people are learning to use the web and its resources as a tool to achieve increased productivity. Users are spending more and more of their work and spare time on the network. Thus it only makes sense for advertisers to target this population.

With the advent of the mass media engines, advertisers have been able to efficiently target their audience to both lower the cost of advertising and to reach the intended audience. Previous to the advent of the net, television, newspapers and magazines, and the radio were the general mediums for advertisements. The use of these forums, although streamlined in recent years, is somewhat clumsy as far as targeting the intended audience.

For instance, have you ever sat down to eat dinner and see a Tampax commercial come on. Or been in the middle of watching a football game and been bombarded with advertisements for toothpaste. Where's the connection? Well, their isn't one. That's the point. There is nothing to say that an armchair sports analyzer isn't a consumer of toothpaste, in fact he is. But wouldn't it be better to try and appeal to this man's hygiene needs in the morning when he's waking up? Of course it would. But that's one of the fundamental problems of television advertising. Target audience.

With net advertising, these problems are simply non-existent. If commercials were free, or at least nearly so, then there would be no problem with inefficient ads. The company could literally saturate the media with images of their product and either people would start buying it, or Congress would pass legislation to ban it. With net advertising, you get the best of both worlds.

Net ads cost virtually nothing to run, and can easily be either sent to people linked to the net, or be placed in strategic "locations" on the web. In this manner, companies can reach the growing audience of net-surfers and solicit their products. Virtual malls also provide an excellent forum for net "browsers" to see the wide array of products available through the web.

In a particular "virtual mall" called Virtumall users can browse through a variety of products, ranging from air freshener to cars to dog biscuits. One particularly interesting shop was the Going Greek Store that specializes in the sales of clothing that's tailored to campus clubs and greek letter organizations. The order form is attractively designed with pictures of almost all of the products so you can see exactly what is being offered.

The cyber mall shopper can enjoy just "walking" through the mall, or if they get the urge to buy, there are usually complete order forms at the bottom of the advertisement. This is a form that is similar to the Home Shopping Network, however on a more personal and easier level. Users of the mall don't even have to wait for the product to change on the screen. They can move through the mall freely, switching the type of products they view or scroll along among the same types of products to find the brand that pleases them.

Most computer companies have web pages set up to provide service and support for the users of their products. Apple Computer's home page is an excellent example of this. Through this format, Apple can display many of their new products to users that are obviously interested in them. Some of the formats include: showrooms that usually give a picture of the product and a brief sales pitch, software updates and lists, and quicktime movies . Consumers can get the information on the products they are interested in without sitting through general purpose, image boosting commercials that really give very little product information. In this way, users can find the information they need quickly and efficiently, which will very likely translate into greater future sales and higher customer satisfaction.

Companies like IBM, advertise a large variety of their hardware, and even provide an option to order over the net, an invaluable tool for impulsive buyers.

On our school systems, the sound capabilities are minimal, but I'm told that the web is in full multimedia context-- graphics, sound, motion--just like television. For years, advertisers have used appealing graphics and sounds to entice an unwary public into purchasing their products. Now, even the net is not exempt from this type of brainwashing.

Some products that can be used over the computer, such as those that are composed entirely of graphics, have even more elaborate selling schemes. For some reason or another, there is no way to censor the material that goes over the lines, which gives companies like Playboy, a brilliant way to advertise their products. By giving "samples" over the internet, the viewer is baited to the product. Furthermore, such an interface allows the user to be interactive and actually participate in many aspects of the magazine, including submitting articles and giving comments to the publisher.

There are many ways that advertisers are using and will use the web in the future to make products both more available and desirable to consumers, but they are far too numerous to go into detail ere. In short, the web is a powerful business tool as well as a source of information and entertainment. Just as the television is able to bring us shows via advertising funds, perhaps the same will soon be true for the net. Maybe in the future, advertising will pay for our entertainment as well as our research.
