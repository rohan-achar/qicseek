Introduction

What is the purpose of prices? Prices allow people to see the costs associated with their actions. In the case of purchasing a good, prices allow an individual to make a rational decision about the value of the good. When prices correctly reflect the costs associated with an action, then the individual can weigh the costs and benefits of their action and make a decision on whether to pursue the action. Presumably if the benefits outweigh the costs, then the individual should purchase the good. In this case, the good that I am referring to is the resources that are available on the Internet and World Wide Web (WWW). Although there are currently prices for using the Internet, the prices are based on monthly subscription fees and one time connection fees. These costs are relatively low, and they do not distinguish between the value an individual has for a prompt connection or for one that is delayed. There is no mechanism that reflects the costs to other individuals brought on by a person making a connection to a server. This inevitably leads to congestion. Gupta, Stahl, and Whinston in their paper Managing the Internet as an Economic System said, "The Internet is already experiencing traffic jams and, given the growth rate...this will become a severe problem if coordinating mechanisms are not designed and implemented." I will argue that it is necessary to begin attaching prices to services in order to relieve the congestion that is greatly increasing on the WWW. The goal of my paper is to provide a foundation for the understanding of how prices may allow the information available on the Internet to be accessed and retrieved more efficiently.

Social Costs

Congestion occurs on the Internet because of too much traffic by individuals on the routers and lines of the Internet. Since there is no pricing mechanism except for the monthly subscription fee, there is no incentive for individuals to reduce their traffic on the Internet. Because the Internet is basically a public good with no costs to individuals associated with the amount of bandwidth they use, individuals typically use more services then they otherwise may have. Over-usage creates externalities on other individuals whose connection and transmission may be delayed (Economides). This problem can be illustrated by the 'Tragedy of the Commons'. During the Middle Ages, villagers shared plots of land called the commons, where everyone could take their sheep to graze. Since there was no mechanism to control the amount of grazing, every individual villager had an incentive to take all of their sheep to graze. This led to an overgrazing of the land. In the case of the Internet and WWW, the lack of a pricing mechanism leads to a similar problem. If I value the information from a server more than Lynn, then I should be able to convey this to the server and receive the information. Prices would enable individuals to measure their preferences for the information available. By attaching prices to the connections of individuals to various servers, WWW users would have a greater incentive to only connect and retrieve information that will be have benefits that are greater then the costs. Prices would provide a mechanism to ensure that the marginal users do not receive the service before users who value it more.

Pricing Mechanisms

I believe the most efficient pricing mechanism, with the lowest social costs, will differentiate between peak usage periods and low usage periods. The majority of the costs associated with WWW usage is in the fixed costs to provide the connections and the backbone. Once the lines and routers are in place, the marginal costs of an additional user are negligible as long as there is available bandwidth. For this reason it would be unreasonable to charge people during non-peak hours, since the marginal costs are so low. MacKie-Mason and Varian, in their paper titled Pricing the Internet called these 'smart markets'. Smart markets would charge people during peak periods. The people who would be willing to pay the most, those who gained the highest utility from a connection, would be the ones who would be allowed to utilize the information available on the server.

There could also be a mechanism that allows people to give a priority to their transmission, during peak periods. Those who give a low priority to their transmission would only be charged a minimal fee, but likewise would not receive prompt connection. The server would only connect when it would not interfere with an other individuals' connection (Mackie-Mason and Varian). As mentioned earlier, the services during non-peak periods would be very cheap, if not free.

Conclusion

Although the value of prices for the services on the Internet are apparent, there is a problem. Currently there is no accounting mechanism to monitor and collect the fees that are related to usage. This is because the transmissions over the Internet are done in packets of data (Bailey). It would not be efficient to charge individuals for their total log on time to a server since most of the time they are not actually transmitting data. Therefore, a mechanism to monitor and then collect the fees would need to be established. Perhaps a system can emerge that charges users on the amount of information that is carried within packets and at the time of the transmission (peak vs. non-peak).

I believe that at the rate of growth on the WWW and Internet, congestion will become more of a problem in the near future. A mechanism has to be established that will aid in the efficient transfer of information of the Internet lines. I feel that by implementing a market driven price structure, as mentioned above, that congestion would be greatly reduced over the WWW. The benefits of the information available on the WWW and Internet are clear (Gaunt). However, the information that is available on the Internet and WWW is only valuable if it is able to be obtained, and in an efficient manner, by the individuals who value it the most. I think prices will help in achieving an efficiency in the retrieval of the resources available on the Internet.
